## **1. Introduction: The Promise That Never Lasts**


You get the promotion.

Or close the deal you worked on for months.

Or move into the apartment you once thought would mean you had “made it.”

For a few days—sometimes a few weeks—the world feels different. There is a quiet lift in your chest. The future looks open, the present feels earned, and life seems to have moved onto a higher plane.

Then something subtle happens.

The promotion becomes normal.

The apartment becomes ordinary.

The achievement dissolves into the background of everyday life.

  
Without noticing exactly when it happened, your expectations reset. The mind quietly recalibrates to its new circumstances and begins looking ahead again: the next target, the next milestone, the next improvement that might finally deliver the lasting sense of satisfaction you thought you were approaching.


Psychologists call this phenomenon the **hedonic treadmill**.

  
In one sentence, the idea is brutally simple: **human beings rapidly adapt to improvements in their circumstances, returning to roughly the same baseline level of satisfaction and beginning the pursuit again.**


The treadmill does not care whether the gain was money, status, comfort, or achievement. The pattern repeats. Life improves; the mind adapts; desire resumes.
  

For many people this realization arrives slowly. It may first appear as a vague sense that accomplishments never feel quite as definitive as expected. Over time the pattern becomes harder to ignore. The victories feel real but strangely temporary. The satisfaction fades faster than it should.

  

Modern life intensifies this experience. For most of human history individuals inherited relatively fixed roles and expectations. The modern world, by contrast, tells us that we must design our own lives—our careers, our ambitions, our definitions of success. It also surrounds us with constant examples of people who appear to be further along the path.

  

The result is a peculiar pressure. We are freer than most humans who have ever lived, but we are also more responsible for finding meaning in a world that rarely stops moving.

  

At some point the question emerges with uncomfortable clarity:

  

**If happiness fades so reliably, what exactly are we supposed to do with our lives?**

  

This is not a modern question. Long before psychologists measured the hedonic treadmill, philosophers and religious traditions were grappling with the same problem.

  

Civilisations noticed the same unsettling pattern: pleasure fades, success loses its intensity, and human desire rarely remains satisfied for long.

  

But rather than treating this as a failure, many cultures built entire philosophies around it. They developed ways of living that did not rely on permanent happiness as the central goal.

  

What emerges, when we look across history, is not one solution but many.

  

Different civilisations discovered the same uncomfortable truth—that happiness is unstable—and each constructed its own **operating system** for dealing with that fact.

  

Understanding those systems does not eliminate the treadmill. But it does something more useful.

  

It shows us the different ways human beings have learned to live with it.

## **2. Classical Antidotes and Their Limits**

  

When philosophers first confronted the problem of unstable happiness, many arrived at a remarkably similar conclusion.

  

If the pursuit of pleasure constantly leads to disappointment, the obvious solution is to change the relationship to desire itself.

  

Across different cultures and centuries, several traditions converged on this insight.

  

The **Buddha** famously taught that suffering arises from craving. Human beings cling to pleasant experiences, resist unpleasant ones, and constantly chase the next source of satisfaction. Liberation, in this view, comes not from satisfying desire but from understanding it and gradually loosening its grip.

  

A similar logic appears in **Epicurus**, the Greek philosopher often misunderstood as a defender of indulgence. In reality, Epicurus believed that the key to tranquility was learning to desire less. Simple pleasures—friendship, modest food, freedom from fear—were sufficient for a peaceful life. The endless pursuit of luxury and status only created unnecessary agitation.

  

The Roman emperor **Marcus Aurelius**, writing in the Stoic tradition, approached the problem from another angle. Much of life lies outside our control, he observed: other people’s opinions, external events, even the eventual loss of everything we possess. Peace comes from narrowing our attention to what we can govern—our judgments, our character, and our response to events.

  

Meanwhile in China, **Lao Tzu** and the Taoist tradition encouraged a similar softening of the will. Instead of forcing the world to conform to our desires, the wise person aligns with the natural flow of things. The Taoist sage acts with minimal striving, avoiding the restless pursuit that makes life unnecessarily tense.

  

Despite their cultural differences, these approaches share a common structural insight.

  

If desire fuels the treadmill, **reduce the power of desire**.

  

Lower expectations.

Detach from outcomes.

Find contentment in simplicity.

  

It is an elegant solution, and it has undeniable power. Entire spiritual traditions have been built on it. Even in modern life, anyone who learns to moderate craving often experiences a dramatic increase in calm and stability.

  

Yet there is also a limitation to this cluster of answers.

  

Despite the variety of names attached to them, they represent **variations of the same fundamental strategy**. They solve the problem by stepping back from it—by reducing the importance of ambition, achievement, and worldly attachment.

  

For many people this approach works beautifully. But it leaves open an uncomfortable question.

  

What if a person does not want to withdraw from striving?

  

What if the desire to build, compete, create, and achieve is not merely a distraction from life but part of what makes life meaningful?

  

Other thinkers confronted the treadmill without abandoning action.

  

**Aristotle**, writing in ancient Greece, proposed a different answer. Human fulfillment does not lie in fleeting pleasure but in **excellence of character and activity**. A flourishing life is one spent developing and exercising our highest capacities—reason, courage, friendship, and skill. Pleasure still fades, but a life structured around mastery and virtue provides a deeper, more durable form of satisfaction.

  

In the **Bhagavad Gita**, one of India’s central philosophical texts, the solution is neither withdrawal nor indulgence but a transformation of motivation. The warrior Arjuna is instructed to act fully in the world—to fight, to perform his duty—but to relinquish attachment to the results. Action continues, but the psychological burden of success and failure loosens.

  

Centuries later, **Friedrich Nietzsche** approached the problem from a radically different angle. Rather than escaping suffering, he argued, one should learn to affirm life in its entirety—including its difficulties. The highest individual does not merely endure hardship but embraces it as part of the creative process of becoming.


Meanwhile, the **Confucian** tradition in East Asia located meaning less in individual psychology and more in social order. A good life emerges through fulfilling one’s roles—parent, child, ruler, citizen—with dignity and care. Ritual, responsibility, and harmony anchor the individual within a stable structure that gives everyday actions significance.


At this point the landscape of responses becomes far richer.

Some traditions reduce desire.

Others transform action.

Others affirm struggle.

Others anchor meaning in social structure.

Yet even these philosophers do not fully explain the diversity of human responses to the fading of happiness.

Each thinker speaks from within a particular cultural world.

To see the full range of answers, we need to zoom out further—not just to philosophers, but to the civilisations that shaped them.

## **3. From Philosophers to Civilisations: Why We Need a Bigger Map**

  

Individual philosophers can offer powerful insights into the human condition. But they rarely stand alone. Behind every thinker lies a cultural world that shaped the questions they asked and the answers that felt plausible.

  

Aristotle did not emerge in a vacuum. His vision of human flourishing—cultivating excellence within a community of citizens—reflects the social world of the Greek city-state. Confucius’s emphasis on harmony and ritual grew from centuries of political disorder in ancient China and the urgent need to restore stable social hierarchies. The Buddha’s diagnosis of suffering was formed in a civilisation deeply preoccupied with cycles of birth, death, and rebirth.

  

Philosophers often appear as solitary figures, but in reality they are the most articulate voices of much larger cultural conversations.

  

Civilisations, over long stretches of history, develop coherent ways of interpreting the human predicament. They accumulate shared intuitions about suffering, success, duty, and meaning. These intuitions are reinforced through religious traditions, political structures, stories, and everyday social practices.

  

Seen from this angle, philosophy becomes less like a set of isolated theories and more like a **distillation of civilisational experience**.

  

This shift in perspective matters for our problem. The hedonic treadmill—the discovery that pleasure fades and satisfaction rarely lasts—is not something any single thinker discovered. It is something entire cultures noticed again and again.

  

And rather than agreeing on one universal solution, civilisations built **different ways of living with that reality**.

  

The Mediterranean world, shaped by vibrant public life and political competition, developed an ideal of human flourishing rooted in excellence and balance. A good life meant cultivating virtue, participating in civic life, and enjoying the pleasures of friendship and beauty while they lasted.

  

In India, where philosophical traditions often focused on impermanence and the cycles of existence, the dominant response was different. Instead of stabilising pleasure, many Indian traditions sought to loosen the individual’s attachment to it entirely.

  

Northern Europe, forged in harsher climates and later shaken by the collapse of religious certainty, moved toward a different solution again: meaning as something created through strength, will, and individual responsibility.

  

Russia, shaped by immense suffering and spiritual intensity, developed a moral imagination in which suffering itself could become a path toward redemption and depth.

  

In East Asia, the answer lay less in individual psychology and more in the creation of harmonious social structures. A stable network of roles, rituals, and obligations provided continuity even when personal emotions fluctuated.

  

Jewish civilisation, forged in centuries of exile and survival, developed a different orientation still: meaning through responsibility, memory, and the continual effort to repair the world.

  

Islamic civilisation redirected the search for satisfaction upward, toward a transcendent order in which human desire finds its proper orientation through submission to the divine.

  

And in Polynesian cultures, shaped by life in close relationship with the ocean and the natural world, identity itself often softened into a broader sense of belonging within living ecosystems.

  

These responses differ dramatically in their details, yet they share a common origin. Each emerged from a culture that had, in its own way, confronted the same unsettling discovery: the pleasures of life are real, but they rarely remain satisfying for long.

  

The important point is not to rank these traditions or to claim that any single one offers the final answer. Each represents a **civilisational strategy**—a way of organising values, expectations, and social life so that human beings can live meaningfully despite the instability of happiness.

  

Looking at these traditions side by side reveals something that is difficult to see from within any one culture. What appears natural or obvious in one civilisation is often only one possible response among many.

  

The Mediterranean emphasis on balanced excellence, the Indian pursuit of detachment, the Northern European celebration of will, the Russian embrace of suffering, the Confucian focus on role and harmony—each represents a different way of standing within the same human condition.

  

If the hedonic treadmill is universal, then these civilisational responses form a kind of **map**. Not a set of instructions, but a landscape of possibilities that humanity has explored over centuries.

  

Understanding that map is the first step toward seeing the problem more clearly—and perhaps toward choosing more deliberately how we want to live within it.
